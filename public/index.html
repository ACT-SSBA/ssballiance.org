What does this mean?
Up until now, you could just deduct the cost of maintaining and improving software in the current year as an expense. But now, because of Section 174, any kind of software development is being treated as research and experimental work that must be spread out over many years (“capitalized”). Even if it's work on an existing product.
So what does that mean, practically?
That means if you paid someone—yourself, even—say, $100,000 last year to develop and maintain software, only $20,000 of that can be taken as an expense each year, and spread over five years.
Wow, that seems bad.
Yeah. Following the previous example, it means your profit—the amount the IRS can tax you on—just increased by a phantom $80,000.
Does it get worse?
Yep. If you live abroad or your developers live abroad, those costs have to be spread out over 15 years.
And you really think we can do something about it?
We can. Big Tech may not be popular on Capitol Hill, but small businesses are much more sympathetic. They just don't know we exist.
If it's repealed, would it be retroactive?
Yes, the repeal bill would make it retroactive. But that isn't going to happen without us speaking up.
